# Overview

This project aims to get us familiar with a camera in my smartphone, such as the relationship between perspective, focus length and the center of projection. 

## Part 1: Selfie - The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way
<figure style="text-align: center; margin: 1em 0;">
  <img src="P1.png" alt="My Image" style="width: 100%">
  <figcaption style="margin-top: 0.5em;"><em>Figure 1.1:</em> 
A distorted selfie on the left and a normal selfie on the right
  </figcaption>
</figure>

I often get an abnormal selfie on the left when I stay too close to the camera, where the cap, glasses and face looks disproportionately stretched and distorted. From my point of view, The field of view in the left image is wider, capturing more background information (such as the slanted red doors), which makes the perspective look curved and exaggerated. However, when I step back and zoom in, the field of view becomes narrower and flatter, which reduces the distortion and results in a more natural-looking portrait.


## Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression
<figure style="text-align: center; margin: 1em 0;">
  <img src="P2.jpg" alt="My Image" style="width: 100%">
  <figcaption style="margin-top: 0.5em;"><em>Figure 2.1:</em> 
A flattened corridor view on the left and the same view with deep depth
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The corridor looks flattened in the left image when I stood far away from the chair and zoom in. The vertical columns and doors are perfectly upright, but the corridor appears shorter and compressed. By contrast, the right image demonstrates a strong sense of depth because the ground and floor converge dramatically. Similar to Part 1, zooming in narrows the field of view, making the architectural perspective more natural by relieving the dramatic depth distortion.

## Part 3: The Dolly Zoom
<figure style="text-align: center; margin: 1em 0;">
  <img src="P3.gif" alt="My Image" style="width: 100%">
  <figcaption style="margin-top: 0.5em;"><em>Figure 3.1:</em> 
An experiment of "Dolly Zoom" effect
  </figcaption>
</figure>

I created a “Dolly Zoom” effect with 12 still photos of a family of ducks. To achieve this, I moved the camera backward while simultaneously zooming in. This technique keeps the subject (the duck) roughly the same size in the frame while the background appears to expand and stretch unnaturally.
